Close reading on The Digital Hegemonies: The Localness of Search Engine Results

The Digital Hegemonies: The Localness of Search Engine Results paper by Ballatore et al. [1] investigated search engines and an assumption that the search engines provide neutral access to information. However, it was found that there was a correlation between locally produced content by wealthier countries and Google searches. This revealed a digital hierarchy where countries such as the United States (US) and Western European regions produce large amounts of information which are often the most visible and most pushed information. There were findings that showed regardless of the large number of people in cites there was relatively low amounts of content about that city in Google search results. Information inequalities are shown very prevalently with this result and can change how certain countries or cities are perceived globally by other countries.

The article begins with looking at Google search results where Google still stands as the most widely used search engine across the world as seen on Global Stats [2]. The aims of the paper investigated firstly; the geographic origin of digital content that appears in the search results is examined. Secondly, the paper aimed to determine the extent to which the local content appeared in searches for the chosen cities. Thirdly, which countries produce their own digital representations, and which countries are defined by external sources. Lastly, investigating the factors which shape the digital geography which include the economic strength, publishing industries, and scientific output.

The paper looked at the Google search results for 188 capital cities which included the many different versions of the search engine such as google.co.uk and google.co.za. The paper investigated the search results in different languages for each respective country. To determine the geographic origin of webpages, Sen et al.'s [3] geoprovenance inference algorithm was used which predicted the country that published a specific URL. Another methodology that was used was measuring the ratio of locally produced search results versus external search results. The last methodology implemented was comparing the search results from Google in the US to the local search results to view the differences between the local and US search results.

The results of the paper provide a critical lens on digital inequalities at the time. This showed how search engines marginalize local digital content and produce a larger spotlight onto dominant information and digital content producers such as the US. The findings of the paper show that there is a need for larger areas of inclusivity in digital spaces which would ensure that regions, regardless of the number of people living in the country, have the ability to digitally represent themselves.

This paper briefly explains that according to the model that was used to collect data and discuss showed that economic development and network connectivity are not enough to make content about that country more local in a Google search. This brings an unanswered question about how search results and the biases could affect tourism and business opportunities in countries and regions that are underrepresented by the search results.

Future research can be done which promotes local content in terms of visibility and this can be where other disciplines contribute. In the current use of artificial intelligence (AI) which can be used as further research in how people in different countries access digital content. This would also bring in other questions and research around topics that the trained algorithm does not respond to without bias or avoidance.

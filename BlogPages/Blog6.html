Internet, Society, and Design Justice

The first article that I will look into is the “Digital Divide Widens” by Maria Tataki and Dimitris Glynos which speaks about the move that happened to online learning during the Covid-19 pandemic [1]. One of the major issues that was prevalent during 2020 was the educational disruption and the lack of facilities and access to the internet which was needed by students to continue with their educational needs during lockdown. A shocking statistic of 82% of students in Sub-Saharan Africa were unable to go online during this period.

A more recent article was released by Statistics South Africa which looked into the percentage gaps of people living in rural areas versus people living in urban areas that had access to resources to receive remote learning [2]. This article confirmed the divide between individuals living in rural areas versus urban areas and the need for the digital transition to take place in areas where individuals were affected the most. As mentioned by Tataki and Glynos, the increasing gap and inequality seen will continue to widen unless plans are created and implemented to provide high-quality and accessible access to the internet.

There are some initiatives being taken such as the “One Laptop Per Child” which aims to teach children digital skills as well as provide lost-cost laptops for the children to use [3]. This is a good start in tackling the digital divide however, in countries such as South Africa, building the infrastructure needed would be the first step that should be taken. An article from 2024 indicates that there is still a divide seen in rural and underserved areas with internet connection through fibre [4]. There are some geographical barriers and installations of fibre does become costly however, there is still an encouragement to the private sector to become involved in creating a country where there is access to high-speed connection.

The second article that I will be looking into is the ethical dilemmas in software engineering by Vincy Davis which speaks about the ethics and principles that are expected to be followed by software engineers [5]. There is a debate that software engineers have to participate in when building and creating products. The software space is often changing so software engineers have to constantly adapt old ethical principles, current ethical principles, and their own ethical standpoints to make ethical decisions. This however needs to have societal considerations that need to be taken into account. The product should adhere to a society where the software product is sustainable as well as catering to a range of different people with different experiences and communities. How the product is received, and future use of the software product needs to be considered.

In some examples of ethical dilemmas in software engineering such as the Tesla car which made use of an assisted feature to the car however the choice to market the car as a fully autopilot car created a situation where an ethical dilemma was involved by not clearly stating that the product was just an enhancement and not a replacement for a driver [6].
